Growth of eligible voters 2001-2017
The total number of eligible voters in the US has grown by 23% from the beginning of the century to 2017.
The growth of eligible Hispanic voters is much higher but it is not obvious in this chart as their voter population is relatively small compared to the national total.
The difference is clear if we compare the growth rate of both groups. From 2001 to 2017, the number of eligible Hispanic voters nearly doubled while the national growth is just 23%.
This means the number of eligible Hispanic voters is increasing 4 times faster than the national average.
The fast growth of Hispanic voters has increased their share of the total voters from 7.8% in 2001 to 12.5% in 2017.
If we zoom in on eligible voters among the Gen Z, the number of both White and Black voters have dropped in recent years.
While Asian voters grow steadily, Hispanic voters have seen a huge increase since 2005, overtaking Black voters in 2010 to become the largest minority group among the Gen Z voters.

States where Hispanic GenZ voters have the biggest impact
States where Hispanic GenZ voters have the biggest impact
The red bars represent the majority voters garnered by Trump in Texas, Florida and Pennsylvania.
The number of Hispanic Gen Z who are eligible to vote in the 2020 elections in those three states, represented by the orange bars, is higher than Trump's majority.
Together the three states can contribute 87 electoral votes to one of the candidates.
The same scenario is seen in another three smaller states won by Trump in 2016 - Arizona, Michigan and Wisconsin - with a total of 37 electoral votes.
For Democrats, only three small states - New Mexico, Nevada, New Hampshire - will see the number of eligible Hispanic voters from Gen Z higher than Clinton's majority in 2016. Together they contributed only 15 electoral votes to Clinton.
